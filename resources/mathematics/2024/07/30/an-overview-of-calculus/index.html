What is gradient?

Look at this image. Any ideas?

The gradient is the slope of the line - how much we go up or down.

But Curves

Of course most lines are curved and so the gradient varies

Gradient of a Point

The question becomes how to find the gradient at a position not between two of them.

The gradient of two points may be defined between two points

\begin{equation} m = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{y(x_2) - y(x_1)}{x_2 - x_1} \label{eqn:twopoint} \end{equation}

Here we can use the average of the two positions to find the gradient at this single position. This of course assumes the gradient found is the gradient of the position in between. This is not always True! But let's say that it is for now.

For ease we will define a single coordinate and then seperate our two points either side of it as such

\begin{equation} m = f'(x) = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{y(x + \frac{\Delta x}{2}) - y(x - \frac{\Delta x}{2})}{\Delta x} \label{eqn:onepoint} \end{equation}

Of interest to us, is what happens to \(m\) as \(\Delta x\) becomes increasingly small.

Differentiation

We find, that for well-behaved functions at least, we are asking what a future value will be based upon a step forward.

\begin{equation} y(x + \frac{\Delta x}{2}) - y(x - \frac{\Delta x}{2}) = m \Delta x \end{equation}

This we can reduce down if we take the limit of \(\Delta x\) to 0.

\begin{equation} dy = \frac{dy}{dx} dx \end{equation}

where

\begin{equation} \frac{dy}{dx} = \lim_{\Delta x \rightarrow 0} m \end{equation}

The operation of 'finding' \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) is called differentiation. In this case we are saying 'differentiate the function y with respect to x'.

We will use the following notation to indicate this.

\begin{equation} \frac{d}{dx}(y) = \frac{dy}{dx} = y^\prime(x) \end{equation}

Playing with differentiation

Usefully we can see

\begin{align} dx &= \frac{dx}{dy}dy \\ dx &= \frac{dx}{dy}\frac{dy}{dx} dx\\ 1 &= \frac{dx}{dy}\frac{dy}{dx} \end{align}

Another useful proof can be found if we consider alternative variables

\begin{align} \frac{dy}{du} du &= dy = \frac{dy}{dx} dx \\ \frac{dy}{du} \frac{du}{dx} dx &= \frac{dy}{dx} dx \\ \therefore \frac{dy}{du} \frac{du}{dx} &= \frac{dy}{dx} \end{align}

This is remarkably useful and is known as the chain rule. We'll use this to 'differentiate' composite functions.

Differentiation as an operation

To use these rules let's define a new operator so that we may perform 'find gradient' in equations and inequalities.

We'll call this new operation, \(\frac{d}{dp}\), which in words means to find the gradient with respect to \(p\).

We can change what variable we are interested in by

\begin{equation} \frac{d}{dx} = \frac{dy}{dx}.\frac{d}{dy} \end{equation}

Composing functions

Conveniently, we may find the gradient of any (well-behaved!) function

Let's consider breaking a polynomial down, say \(x^6 = (x^3)^2\)

\begin{align} x^{nm} &= (x^n)^m \\ \frac{d}{dx}(x^{nm}) &= \frac{d}{dx}((x^n)^m) \\ &= \frac{dp}{dx}\frac{d}{dp}(p^m) \\ &= \frac{dp}{dx}mp^{m-1} \end{align}

where \(p = x^n\) so

\begin{align} \frac{dp}{dx} &= nx^{n-1} \\ \frac{d}{dx}(x^{nm}) &= nx^{n-1}mp^{m-1} \\ &= nx^{n-1}m(x^n)^{m-1} \\ &= nmx^{nm-1} \end{align}

Unsurprisingly this works out quite well. Let's consider an equation this time.

\begin{align} y &= x^\frac{n}{m} \\ y^m &= x^n \\ \frac{d}{dx}(y^m) &= \frac{d}{dx}(x^n) \\ \frac{dy}{dx}\frac{d}{dy}(y^m) &= \frac{d}{dx}(x^n) \\ \frac{dy}{dx}my^{m-1} &= nx^{n-1} \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{m}{n}x^{\frac{m}{n} - 1} \end{align}

Perhaps more interesting is we can solve more complex functions by breaking them into one of the five functions we recognise using this. For instance

\begin{equation*} y = \arcsin{x}\\ \therefore \sin{y}= x\\ \frac{d}{dx}(\sin{y})= \frac{d}{dx}(x)\\ \frac{dy}{dx}.\frac{d}{dy}(\sin{y}) = \frac{d}{dx}(x) \\ \frac{dy}{dx}.\cos{y} = 1\\ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\cos{y}} \end{equation*}

This is fine but in this case we can put the result in terms of \(x\) using the trignometry identity \(\sin^2{x} +\cos^2{x} = 1\). This gives

\begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \sin^2{y}}}\\ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \end{equation*}

Multiplying Functions

Consider a function

\begin{equation} f &= uv \end{equation}

If \(f\) varies, how do \(u\) and \(v\) vary?

Let's consider an area changing this time. \(dx = u dv + v du\) Here we can see \begin{align} d(uv) &= \frac{d(uv)}{dx} dx \ u dv + v du + du dv &= \frac{d(uv)}{dx} dx

u \frac{dv}{dx}dx + v \frac{du}{dx} dx &= \frac{d(uv)}{dx} dx \ \therefore \frac{d}{dx}(uv) = \frac{du}{dx}v + u\frac{dv}{dx} \end{align}
